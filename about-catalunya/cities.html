Introduction

Catalunya is a compact region — roughly the size of Belgium — yet it contains an extraordinary variety of cities and towns, from a great European capital to small medieval market towns. Each reflects a different facet of Catalan history and character. All of the cities below are reachable by car or public transport from Tamariu.

Barcelona

The Catalan capital and one of Europe's great cities. Home to Gaudí's Sagrada Família, the Gothic Quarter, the Rambla, Park Güell, Barceloneta beach and world-class food and nightlife. About 2 hours from Tamariu by car.

Girona

The provincial capital, just 45 minutes from Tamariu. Medieval walls, a soaring cathedral, the Jewish quarter (Call), the River Onyar with its colourful houses, and a remarkable food scene including the world-renowned restaurant El Celler de Can Roca.

Tarragona

Roman Tarraco — a UNESCO World Heritage Site. An amphitheatre on the seafront, a massive circus, Augustan city walls, and a remarkably intact Roman street grid. Also known for its castellers and its annual human towers festival.

Figueres

Home of Salvador Dalí and his extraordinary Teatre-Museu Dalí — the most visited museum in Spain after the Prado. The town itself is modest, but the museum is unmissable. About 1 hour north of Tamariu.

Lleida

The western capital of Catalunya, dominated by the dramatic Seu Vella — a medieval cathedral built on a hilltop Roman fortress. A less-visited city with a relaxed pace and excellent local food traditions including fine lamb and river fish.

Vic

An inland market town famous for its medieval centre, its baroque cathedral frescoes by Josep Maria Sert, and above all its embotits — the finest cured meats and sausages in Catalunya. The Saturday market is one of the best in the region.

Sitges

A charming coastal town south of Barcelona, known for its beautiful beaches, modernista architecture, vibrant LGBTQ+ community and one of Europe's most celebrated carnivals in February. A relaxed contrast to Barcelona's intensity.

Besalú

One of the best-preserved medieval towns in Spain. Its 12th-century fortified bridge over the River Fluvià is instantly recognisable. The town retains its Jewish quarter, Romanesque churches and ancient street plan — virtually unchanged in 800 years.

Montserrat

Not a city but an unmissable destination — a serrated mountain massif rising dramatically from the plain, topped by a Benedictine monastery housing the revered Black Madonna. The views are extraordinary and the hiking trails outstanding.

Cadaqués

The most beautiful village on the Costa Brava — a whitewashed fishing village on a rocky bay accessible only by a winding mountain road. Dalí lived here for decades. Artists, writers and travellers have been drawn to its light and isolation since the 1960s.

Olot

Capital of the volcanic Garrotxa region — a landscape of extinct volcanoes, beech forests and medieval villages. Excellent hiking in the Zona Volcànica Natural Park. The town itself has a remarkable early modernista architectural heritage.

Peratallada

A perfectly preserved medieval village carved from golden sandstone, just 20 minutes from Tamariu. The castle, walls and cobbled streets are essentially unchanged from the 11th century. An ideal afternoon excursion with excellent restaurants.

Day Trips from Tamariu

Tamariu's position on the Costa Brava places it within easy reach of much of what Catalunya has to offer. Girona is just 45 minutes away and well worth a full day. Figueres and Cadaqués are an hour north. The medieval villages of the Baix Empordà — Pals, Peratallada, Ullastret with its Iberian ruins — are all within 30 minutes. Barcelona makes a long but entirely manageable day trip at around two hours each way.
