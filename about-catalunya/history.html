Prehistoric and Pre-Roman Catalunya

The territory that is now Catalunya has been inhabited since the Palaeolithic period — evidence of human presence dates back more than 200,000 years. By the first millennium BC, Iberian peoples had established sophisticated settlements along the coast and inland valleys. The Greeks founded the colony of Empúries (Emporion) on the Costa Brava around 575 BC — its remarkable ruins survive and are visible from Tamariu. The Carthaginians were also active in the region, and it was through here that Hannibal is said to have marched his army towards Rome in 218 BC.

Roman Hispania (218 BC – 5th century AD)

Rome invaded the Iberian Peninsula in 218 BC and made the northeast its base of operations. Tarraco (modern Tarragona) became the capital of the province of Hispania Citerior and one of the most important cities in the western Roman Empire. Its amphitheatre, circus and city walls survive to this day and are designated UNESCO World Heritage Sites. Roman roads, bridges and aqueducts shaped the landscape of Catalunya for centuries. Latin — the language of the colonisers — evolved into Catalan over the following centuries of Visigothic rule and early medieval settlement.

Visigothic Rule and Moorish Conquest

With the collapse of the Western Roman Empire in the 5th century, the Visigoths took control of the peninsula. Their capital was Toledo but they maintained a significant presence in Hispania Citerior. In 711 AD, Moorish forces crossed from North Africa and within seven years had conquered most of the Iberian Peninsula, including what is now Catalunya. However, Moorish control of the northeast was relatively brief — Frankish forces under Charlemagne recaptured Barcelona in 801 AD, establishing the Hispanic March as a buffer zone between the Frankish Empire and Al-Andalus.

The County of Barcelona and the Birth of Catalunya

From the Carolingian March, a series of independent counties gradually emerged. The County of Barcelona grew dominant under Guifré el Pilós (Wilfred the Hairy, c.840–897), who is considered the founding father of Catalunya. Legend holds that when Wilfred was wounded in battle, the Frankish king drew four fingers of blood across his golden shield — creating the four red stripes on gold that remain the Catalan flag (la Senyera) to this day.

In 988, Count Borrell II broke ties with the Frankish crown, effectively making the County of Barcelona an independent state. The name Catalunya first appears in documents from around 1115.

The Crown of Aragon (1137–1469)

In 1137, the County of Barcelona united with the Kingdom of Aragon through marriage, forming the Crown of Aragon — a powerful federation that would expand to include Valencia, the Balearic Islands, Sicily, Sardinia, Naples and briefly Athens. The 13th and 14th centuries were a golden age for Catalan commerce, culture and language. Barcelona became one of the great trading cities of the Mediterranean, and the Corts Catalanes (Catalan Parliament) was among the earliest representative assemblies in Europe.

Union with Castile and the War of Succession

In 1469, Ferdinand II of Aragon married Isabella I of Castile, uniting the two kingdoms under one crown. Though it was a personal union rather than a merger, Catalan institutions and laws were gradually eroded over the following centuries. The catastrophic turning point came with the War of the Spanish Succession (1701–1714). Catalunya backed the Habsburg claimant against the Bourbon Philip V. On 11 September 1714, Barcelona fell after a year-long siege. Philip V abolished all Catalan institutions, banned the Catalan language in official life, and imposed Castilian law — a date Catalans commemorate as their National Day to this day.

The 19th Century: Industrialisation and the Renaixença

Catalunya was the first industrialised region of the Iberian Peninsula, developing a powerful textile industry from the 1820s onwards. The wealth generated by industry fuelled both social conflict and a cultural renaissance. The Renaixença (Rebirth) movement, from the mid-19th century, revived Catalan language and literature after two centuries of suppression. Poets, linguists and historians worked to codify and celebrate Catalan culture. Barcelona's remarkable Eixample (Extension) district, designed by Ildefons Cerdà, was laid out from 1860 — one of the great achievements of 19th-century urban planning.

The 20th Century: Republic, Civil War and Dictatorship

The early 20th century saw the rise of Catalan nationalism alongside workers' movements and anarchism. The Second Spanish Republic (1931–1936) granted Catalunya the Statute of Autonomy, restoring the Generalitat — its regional government. The Spanish Civil War (1936–1939) was catastrophic. Barcelona was a Republican stronghold and suffered intense bombardment; the city fell in January 1939. Under Francisco Franco (1939–1975), Catalan was banned, cultural organisations dissolved, and thousands of Catalans were imprisoned or executed. The language survived only in private homes and the Catholic Church.

Democracy, Autonomy and the Independence Question

Franco's death in 1975 and the transition to democracy brought a rapid rehabilitation of Catalan identity. The 1979 Statute of Autonomy restored the Generalitat and made Catalan the co-official language. Catalan was reintroduced in schools and public life, and the culture experienced a second renaissance. In the 21st century, a growing independence movement has defined Catalan politics. A disputed referendum in October 2017 and its contested aftermath remain unresolved — but they reflect a centuries-old question about identity, sovereignty and belonging that Catalunya has never stopped asking.
