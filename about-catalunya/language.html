Origins and History

Catalan (català) is a Romance language descended directly from Vulgar Latin. It developed in the eastern Pyrenean region during the 9th and 10th centuries alongside the emergence of the County of Barcelona. Closely related to Occitan and French, it shares more with those languages than with Castilian Spanish, despite centuries of political union with Spain.

Catalan enjoyed a golden age during the 12th–15th centuries, producing great literature, poetry and legal texts. Ramon Llull (1232–1316) was among the first European writers to use a vernacular language for philosophy and science.

Following the War of the Spanish Succession (1714), Philip V abolished Catalan institutions and suppressed the language in public life. The 19th century Renaixença (Renaissance) movement revived Catalan literature and cultural pride. Under Franco (1939–1975), Catalan was banned in schools, government and public spaces — yet it survived in homes, churches and whispered conversations. Since the restoration of democracy, Catalan has been fully rehabilitated and is today the co-official language of Catalunya, Valencia (as Valencian) and the Balearic Islands.

Today approximately 10 million people speak Catalan as their first language. It is the official language of Andorra and is spoken in parts of southern France, Sardinia and the Valencian Community. It is the only official language of Andorra.
