Unusual and Surprising Facts about Catalunya

Catalunya is a place of deep contradictions and endearing peculiarities. Beneath the sophisticated exterior of Barcelona and the quiet beauty of the Costa Brava lies a culture full of surprising customs, eccentric traditions and facts that visitors rarely expect. Here are some of the most memorable.

The Caga Tió — The Christmas Log

Every Catalan household has a Tió de Nadal — a hollow log with a smiling painted face and a little red hat. From 8th December, children feed it scraps of food each night. On Christmas Eve, they beat it with sticks while singing a song, demanding it "defecate" gifts. It does — sweets, nuts and small presents hidden inside. Possibly the most unusual Christmas tradition in Europe.

El Caganer — The Christmas Defecator

Catalan nativity scenes (pessebres) include all the standard figures — but tucked away in a corner is the caganer: a small figure squatting with his trousers around his ankles. Traditionally a peasant, modern caganers come as politicians, celebrities, footballers and even popes. Far from being considered rude, the caganer is considered a symbol of good luck and equality — he fertilises the earth.

Catalan is Spoken in Four Countries

Catalan is the official language of Andorra and is spoken in parts of France (Roussillon), Italy (Sardinia's Alghero) and Spain (Catalunya, Valencia, Balearic Islands). With around 10 million speakers, it is the 8th most spoken language in the European Union.

Human Towers are UNESCO Heritage

Castellers — human towers reaching 10 storeys — were inscribed on UNESCO's Intangible Cultural Heritage list in 2010. The tradition is entirely non-competitive in spirit; teams (colles castelleres) judge themselves by their own standards. A tower is not complete until the enxaneta — always a small child — reaches the top.

Catalunya Was Not Always Part of Spain

The political union of Catalunya with Castile came through marriage in 1469, but Catalunya retained its own institutions, laws and parliament for another 245 years — until they were abolished after the War of the Spanish Succession in 1714. Catalans have never fully accepted that this was a legitimate union.

The Catalan Flag is One of the Oldest in the World

The Senyera — four red stripes on gold — is first documented in the 12th century, making it one of the oldest flags in continuous use anywhere in the world. Legend attributes it to Wilfred the Hairy, though historians are somewhat sceptical of the romantic story involving royal blood on a golden shield.

Barcelona Has Its Own Time Zone Quirk

Geographically, Barcelona sits at roughly the same longitude as London and should logically be in the UTC+0 time zone. However, Franco aligned Spain with Nazi Germany's time zone during World War II, moving the clocks forward one hour — where they have remained ever since. Catalans go to bed later than almost anyone in Europe partly because their "midnight" is geographically closer to 11pm.

The World's First Literary Prize for Women

The Jocs Florals, revived in Barcelona in 1859 during the Renaixença, were the world's first literary competition to award prizes specifically to women poets. The tradition of awarding a rose to the winner survives in Sant Jordi celebrations today.

Gaudí Was Almost Nobody

When Antoni Gaudí died in 1926 — hit by a tram because he looked so shabby that taxi drivers assumed he was a beggar and refused to stop — he was buried in the crypt of the Sagrada Família, which had then been under construction for 44 years. It is still unfinished today, over a century after his death, and is the most visited building in Spain.

The Correfoc: Running With Fire

The correfoc (fire run) is perhaps the most visceral Catalan tradition. Groups dressed as devils charge through streets with handheld fireworks and fire-spewing dragon figures, while crowds dance among the sparks. Participants are advised to wear old, synthetic-free clothing. It is terrifying, exhilarating and utterly Catalan.

Catalan Sardines are Not What You Think

The feast of Sant Joan (Midsummer, 23–24 June) is celebrated across Catalunya with bonfires, fireworks and the communal grilling of sardines on the beach — espinagada de sardines. In Tamariu and every coastal village, families gather on the beach at midnight to eat sardines and watch the fireworks. It is among the most magical nights of the Catalan year.

Three Great Artists Born in Catalunya

Salvador Dalí (Figueres), Joan Miró (Barcelona), and Antoni Gaudí (Reus) were all Catalan. Their work collectively spans surrealism, abstract expressionism and organic architecture — and all three drew directly on Catalan landscape, culture and identity as the source of their vision.

A note on identity: When visiting Catalunya, be aware that many locals feel strongly Catalan first and Spanish second — or not Spanish at all. Addressing people in Catalan (even just hola and gràcies) is always warmly appreciated, as is showing genuine curiosity about Catalan culture and history. The region's complex identity is not a political abstraction — it is lived daily, and visitors who engage with it respectfully will find an exceptionally warm welcome.
