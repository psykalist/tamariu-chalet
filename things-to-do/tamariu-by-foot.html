Tamariu by Foot

You do not need the car every day. Tamariu sits on one of the finest stretches of the camí de ronda — the old coastguard path that threads the whole Costa Brava — and in summer three separate bus services put the surrounding villages, beaches and towns within easy reach. Add the dive centre, kayaks and boards working straight off the beach, and there is a week's worth of days here. What follows is seven of them, each planned from breakfast to dinner, with the car left exactly where it is.

Before you set out

Times are indicative. Every service here is seasonal and the operators revise their timetables each year, sometimes mid-season. The schedules below show the shape of a realistic day using the current pattern — always confirm the day's departures on the official links given with each option before committing to a plan.

The coastal paths are currently open along all the routes described here. Sections do close from time to time for safety works, so if you are reading this well ahead of your stay, do ask us for the latest position when you arrive.

Day one · Easiest

The Julivia Bus Day

One ticket, six departures, hop on and off as often as you like

Effort Very easy Season Mid-July to late Sept Cost ~€6.50 adult · €3 child 4–12 Walking Under 2 km

The Julivia is Palafrugell's summer tourist bus, and it is the single best thing on this page for anyone who wants to see a lot without walking far. It runs a long loop taking in Palafrugell town, Tamariu, the Sant Sebastià lighthouse, Llafranc, the campsites, El Golfet beach and the Cap Roig botanical gardens. The whole circuit takes about ninety minutes.

Crucially it is a hop-on, hop-off ticket, valid all day and as many times as you like — so the sensible approach is not to ride the loop but to use it as a shuttle between three or four places you actually want to spend time in. Buy on board, at the Palafrugell tourist office or at the Sarfa bus station.

A day that works

  • 09:00Breakfast at the chalet, then the short walk down into Tamariu village.
  • 10:00Pick up the Julivia at the Tamariu stop. Buy the day ticket from the driver.
  • 10:30Hop off at the Far de Sant Sebastià. The highest lighthouse on the Costa Brava, with an Iberian settlement, an 18th-century watchtower and a view down the whole coast. Allow an hour.
  • 11:45Back on the bus down to Llafranc. Swim from the sandy bay, coffee on the promenade.
  • 13:15Ride on to Calella de Palafrugell for a long lunch among the arcaded fishermen's houses at Port Bo.
  • 15:30Hop off at Cap Roig — clifftop botanical gardens above the sea, and the best-value ticket in the area. Two hours is right.
  • 17:45Catch a late loop back towards Tamariu.
  • 19:00Swim off Tamariu beach before dinner, and eat on the seafront.

Worth knowing

  • Six departures a day — roughly three in the morning and three in the afternoon. Gaps between buses are long, so plan around the timetable rather than assuming you can leave when you like.
  • Download the Julivia bus app — it carries the live route, stop locations, times and a commentary on the town's history.
  • Cap Roig needs its own ticket, separate from the bus fare. In summer it also hosts a well-known music festival, so evenings can be busy.

Day two · The classic

The Coast Walk to Llafranc

Tamariu to the lighthouse and down into Llafranc, on the GR92

Distance ~5.8 km one way Walking time 1½–2 hrs each way Effort Moderate — steep, rocky Season Year round

This is the walk people remember. The GR92 long-distance path follows the camí de ronda south out of Tamariu, climbing hard through pines to the Sant Sebastià headland before dropping into Llafranc. On the way it passes above Cala Pedrosa, a shingle cove with no road access at all, reachable only by this path or by boat.

Be honest with yourself about the terrain. It is not a promenade: there are long flights of rough steps, exposed rocky sections and a stretch near Tamariu where you scramble over boulders. Sturdy shoes rather than sandals, and a lot more water than you think you need.

A day that works

  • 08:00Early breakfast. The climb is much kinder before the sun gets high.
  • 08:30Leave from the southern end of Tamariu beach — follow the signs for the camí de ronda and the red-and-white GR waymarks.
  • 09:20Detour down to Cala Pedrosa for a swim with almost nobody else there. Steep in and steeper out.
  • 10:30Top out at the Far de Sant Sebastià — lighthouse, watchtower, chapel and a panorama over the whole coastline. Coffee at the terrace.
  • 11:30Descend the zigzags into Llafranc. Long swim from the sandy beach.
  • 13:30Lunch on the Llafranc promenade.
  • 15:30Either walk the path back, or — in July and August — take Bus 60 from Llafranc round to Tamariu and save your knees.
  • 17:00Back at the chalet with the afternoon still going.

Worth knowing

  • Walk it one way, ride back. In high season Bus 60 links Llafranc and Tamariu hourly, which turns a demanding out-and-back into a very manageable day.
  • Out of season there is no bus, so budget for the full return on foot — around four hours of walking plus stops.
  • No shops on the path. Water, hat, sun cream — the exposed sections have no shade at all.

Day three · The turquoise one

The Walk North to Aiguablava

Tamariu to Aigua Xelida and on to the most photographed bay on the coast

Distance ~9 km round trip Walking time 4–5 hrs with stops Effort Moderate — steep in places Season Year round

Head north instead and the camí de ronda takes you first to Aigua Xelida — a twenty-five metre scrap of pebble and rock hemmed in by extraordinary formations, and one of the quietest swims within reach of the village — and then on around the headland to Aiguablava, the pale-sand bay whose water genuinely is the colour it appears in the photographs.

It is a gentler proposition than the Llafranc walk, though still steep in sections, and there is a proper reward at the far end: lunch at Toc al Mar, directly on the sand, then a swim before turning back.

A day that works

  • 08:30Breakfast, then down to the northern end of Tamariu beach where the path starts to climb.
  • 09:15Drop into Aigua Xelida. First swim of the day, almost certainly alone.
  • 10:30Continue north on the camí de ronda around the headland, with the path opening onto one cove view after another.
  • 11:45Arrive at Fornells and its small harbour, then round to Aiguablava itself.
  • 12:15Swim in the turquoise. The water is calm and shallow — the best snorkelling is around the rocky edges.
  • 13:30Lunch at Toc al Mar on the sand, or up at the Parador on the headland for the view.
  • 15:30Walk back the way you came, in better light and with the sea on your left.
  • 17:30Back in Tamariu for a final swim.

Worth knowing

  • Book Toc al Mar ahead in summer — it is something of a Costa Brava institution and fills up well in advance.
  • There is no easy bus back. Aiguablava sits in the Begur municipality, so the Palafrugell services do not help you here — the return is on foot, or a taxi. Plan the energy accordingly.
  • Time it with the light. Aiguablava's colour is at its most absurd in late morning, with the sun high and behind you.

Day four · Cove hopping

The Begur Beach Bus

Walk to Aiguablava, then let the shuttle do the rest of the coves

Walk out ~2.2 km · 35–40 min Season Mid-June to mid-Sept Cost ~€1.50 single · ~€3 day ticket Effort Easy — but a steep start

Begur council runs a summer shuttle from Plaça Forgas in the town centre out to Sa Riera, Sa Tuna, Fornells and Aiguablava. It solves the single worst problem on this coast — the Begur coves have tiny, expensive car parks that fill by mid-morning — and a day ticket costs about the price of an hour's parking.

The neat trick is that you do not need a car to join it. Aiguablava is only about 2.2 km from the chalet, and the shuttle stops there. So you walk out, buy a day ticket on board, and spend the rest of the day hopping between coves that most people are queuing to park at.

It is a short distance but not a flat one — the road climbs steadily out of the valley before dropping to the bay, so allow closer to forty minutes than thirty and take it gently in the heat. The compensation is that the return leg, at the end of a long day, is mostly downhill.

This is the quick, direct route out — quite different from the long coastal walk in day three, which reaches the same bay the scenic way round in a couple of hours. Take the road today; save the camí de ronda for when you have the whole day to give it.

A day that works

  • 09:00Breakfast, then set off on foot from the chalet towards Aiguablava — about 2.2 km by road, with a steady climb before the descent to the bay.
  • 09:40Arrive at Aiguablava. First swim of the day in the turquoise while the bay is still quiet.
  • 11:00Pick up the beach bus at the cove and buy a day ticket from the driver.
  • 11:30Across to Sa Tuna, a tiny pebble cove ringed with blue-and-white fishermen's houses. Deep, clear water.
  • 13:00Lunch at the water's edge in Sa Tuna.
  • 14:45Shuttle on to Sa Riera — the largest of the Begur beaches, and the one that takes a family for a whole afternoon.
  • 17:30Up to Begur itself. Climb to the castle ruins for the view over the entire coast at golden hour.
  • 19:00Dinner in the old town, then the last shuttle down to Aiguablava and the walk home — or a short taxi if legs have had enough.

Worth knowing

  • Take the day ticket, not singles. It pays for itself on the second hop and removes any anxiety about doubling back.
  • Check the last departure before you settle in for dinner in Begur — the evening service finishes earlier than you would expect, and the walk home from Aiguablava is unlit.
  • The walk out has no pavement in places and climbs steadily. Fine in daylight and quiet enough early, but keep children close and wear something visible on the way back.
  • Go early if it is hot. The climb is on open road with little shade — a nine o'clock start is a very different walk from a midday one.
  • Reduced fares apply for children, and for over-65s; under-fives generally travel free.
  • Begur's old town is steep. Cobbles and stairs everywhere, especially up to the castle — not the day for smooth soles.

Day five · Town day

Bus 60 to Palafrugell

The market, the old centre and a proper Catalan town lunch

Season July & August Frequency Roughly hourly Cost ~€2 single Effort Easy

Bus 60 is the summer service that finally connects Tamariu to the outside world without a car, looping between Palafrugell bus station, Llafranc and Tamariu roughly once an hour through the morning and again through the late afternoon. A single fare is around €2, and integrated ATM Girona travel cards bring that down considerably if you are using it repeatedly.

Palafrugell is the working town behind the pretty villages — larger, less polished and far more interesting for it. If you can time this for a Sunday, the weekly market is one of the best in the Baix Empordà.

A day that works

  • 09:00Walk down to the Tamariu bus stop and pick up the first Bus 60 of the morning.
  • 09:30Arrive at Palafrugell bus station and walk into the centre.
  • 10:00The market if it is Sunday; otherwise the daily covered market and the streets around Plaça Nova.
  • 11:30Museu del Suro, the cork museum — a genuinely good account of the industry that built this whole area.
  • 13:30Lunch in town. This is where you get the menú del dia at local prices rather than seafront ones.
  • 15:30Can Mario — contemporary sculpture in a converted cork factory, with the modernista water tower alongside.
  • 17:00Bus 60 back towards the coast.
  • 17:45Home in time for a swim.

Worth knowing

  • Mind the midday gap. Bus 60 typically runs mornings and late afternoons with a break in between — check the return times before you settle into a long lunch.
  • Bus 30 is the sister service, running every half hour between Palafrugell, Calella and Llafranc — useful for combining a town morning with a beach afternoon.
  • Out of July and August these services do not run, and you are back to the year-round regional buses from Palafrugell.

Day six · When it rains

The Rainy Day

Museums in Palafrugell, or push on to Girona for the day

Effort Easy Mostly Indoors Girona ~1 hr by bus Note Museums shut Mondays

It does rain on the Costa Brava, and when it does the coast empties. Two good options, depending on how far you want to go.

The short version keeps you in Palafrugell: the cork museum and Can Mario are both properly indoor, twenty minutes apart on foot, and a long lunch fills the gap between them. The long version continues to Girona — around an hour by regional bus — where the cathedral, the Jewish quarter and the colonnaded riverfront make an easy day out of the weather.

A day that works — the Girona version

  • 09:00Bus 60 (or a lift) to Palafrugell bus station.
  • 09:45Regional Moventis/Sarfa coach to Girona — around an hour, and a comfortable ride.
  • 11:00Coffee in the old town, then the cathedral and its vast single-nave interior.
  • 12:30The Call, Girona's medieval Jewish quarter — narrow, covered, and at its most atmospheric in bad weather.
  • 14:00Lunch. Girona takes its food seriously and the options are a step up from the coast.
  • 16:00The Arab baths and the city walls, or the art museum if it is still coming down.
  • 18:00Coach back to Palafrugell.
  • 19:30Home, with the weather forgotten.

Worth knowing

  • Museu del Suro is closed on Mondays. It generally opens mornings and late afternoons Tuesday to Saturday, and mornings only on Sundays and holidays — with a long midday break, as everywhere here.
  • Can Mario keeps similar split hours, Monday to Saturday, mornings only on Sundays.
  • Check the last coach back from Girona before you go. Services thin out considerably in the evening and outside high season.
  • The camí de ronda after rain is slippery on the rock sections and best avoided the same day.

Day seven · On the water

A Day on the Water

Everything here launches from Tamariu beach — no car, no transfer

Effort Your choice Season Roughly Easter to October Walk to start Down to the beach Booking Essential in summer

The best argument for staying in Tamariu rather than one of the bigger resorts is that the sea is genuinely on the doorstep. The dive centre, the kayak operator and the board hire all work off the beach itself — a ten-minute walk from the chalet — and the coastline immediately north and south is some of the most dramatic on the Costa Brava, riddled with caves and cove entrances you simply cannot reach on foot.

The single most remarkable thing out there is the Cova d'en Gispert, between Tamariu and Aiguablava — at around 150 metres it is the longest sea cave on this coast, and both the kayak tours and the boat trips head for it.

A day that works

  • 08:45Walk down to the beach and check in with the kayak operator. Dry bag, buoyancy aid, quick briefing.
  • 09:30Push off on the half-day guided sea kayak, heading along the cliffs with snorkelling stops and cave entrances on the way.
  • 12:30Back on the sand, pleasantly wrecked.
  • 13:00Lunch on the Tamariu seafront — you have earned the long one.
  • 15:30Hire a paddle board for the flat afternoon water and potter round the bay under your own steam.
  • 17:00Mask and fins over the reefs at either end of the beach — octopus, wrasse, and the protected Posidonia meadows just offshore.
  • 19:30Dinner fifty metres from where you have spent the entire day.

What runs from the beach

Scuba diving

Stolli's Divebase · Tamariu

A long-established dive centre working straight off Tamariu beach, running courses for complete beginners through to guided dives for the experienced. The house reef can be dived all day; boat dives generally go out once or twice daily.

The local sites are excellent — the canyons off Tamariu thick with sea fans, the Furió d'Aigua Xelida for moray eels and lobster, and the Gispert cave itself.

Sea kayaking

Kayaking Costa Brava · Tamariu

Guided half-day excursions leaving from the beach and following the cliffs, with stops to snorkel and to paddle into caves and narrow passages that are invisible from land. No experience needed — the guides pitch it to the group.

Double kayaks are the usual choice for couples and for a parent with a child. Book ahead in July and August.

Paddle boarding

Beach hire · summer season

Boards hire by the hour from the beach in summer. The bay is sheltered and the water usually glassy in the morning and late afternoon, which makes it a forgiving place to learn.

Stay inside the marked swimming area's seaward buoys and keep an eye on the wind — see the caution below.

Boat trips

From Tamariu & Llafranc harbour

Coastal excursions run along the shoreline between Palamós and Tamariu, taking in the coves and caves from the water. Some operators use a larger boat for the open stretches and a zodiac for nosing into the tight cave entrances.

Llafranc has the nearest proper harbour and the widest choice — reachable by Bus 60 in summer, or on foot via day two.

Worth knowing

  • Book ahead in high season. The kayak tours and dive slots fill days in advance through July and August.
  • Respect the Tramuntana. The north wind can arrive fast and hard, and it turns a calm bay into a difficult paddle very quickly. If the operators are not going out, do not go out.
  • Water shoes help. The entry points around the rocky ends of the bay are sharp underfoot.
  • The Posidonia meadows are protected. Wonderful to snorkel over, but do not anchor in them or pull at them.
  • Lifeguard in summer on Tamariu beach, with flags showing the day's sea conditions.

What to take

  • Proper shoes for the coastal path. The camí de ronda has rough steps, loose stone and rock scrambles. Trainers at minimum; sandals will spoil the day.
  • More water than seems necessary. There is no shade on the exposed sections and no shop between villages.
  • Cash for the buses. Drivers sell tickets on board, and small notes are appreciated.
  • Swim things on every walk. Every one of these routes passes a cove you will want to get into.
  • A printed or downloaded timetable. Phone signal is patchy on the headlands, exactly where you will want to check the return bus.
