Local Beaches & Coves

One of the great luxuries of staying in Tamariu is that you are not limited to one beach. Within half an hour's drive there are a dozen quite different stretches of coast — hidden coves reached only on foot, long pine-backed sands you could lose a family on, and pretty fishing villages where lunch is served a few metres from the water. Here is our honest guide to each of them: what it's like, where to eat, and how far it is from the chalet.

Walking Distance

On the Doorstep

Three beaches you can reach from the chalet without ever starting the car — one at the bottom of the hill, two along the coastal footpath.

Our Home Beach

Platja de Tamariu

Palafrugell · Blue Flag · fine sand

From the chalet 10 min walk By car 5 min

A perfect crescent of pale sand about 175 metres long, held between two pine-covered headlands and lined with a low, unhurried promenade. Tamariu has never been allowed to grow — there are no high-rise blocks, no traffic on the front, and the fishing boats are still pulled up at the north end. The water is exceptionally clear and shelves gently, which makes it one of the safest family beaches on this stretch of coast.

It faces east, so mornings are bright and sheltered and the afternoons fall pleasantly into shade under the cliffs. Snorkelling around the rocks at either end is superb — you'll find wrasse, octopus and the occasional cuttlefish within a few metres of the sand. Sunbeds, kayaks and paddleboards can be hired on the beach in season, and there's a small diving centre.

Family-friendlySnorkellingRestaurants on the sandKayak hireLifeguard in season
P

Parking

Free & paidMixed — free further up the hill

The seafront and the streets immediately behind the beach are blue zone (paid) from roughly June to September, and there is a small manned car park by the beach. Spaces near the sand are gone by mid-morning in July and August.

Free option: park higher up on the road climbing out of the village towards Aiguablava and walk down — five to ten minutes, and free all year. Outside the summer months the blue zone is free too.

Where to eat

  • El Palanquí — the star of the seafront. Terrace at the water's edge; order the paella or the lobster rice. Book ahead in summer.
  • Royal Restaurant — a reliable beachfront institution; excellent croquettes and mussels, family paella with a day's notice.
  • Bar Ona — the liveliest spot at the beach edge, all day from breakfast to cocktails.
  • Mossec — the village's best-kept secret, loved by locals for its arròs mar i muntanya.

Hidden Cove · No Road Access

Cala Pedrosa

Palafrugell · pebble & shingle

From Tamariu ~2 km on foot Walking time 45–50 min each way By car not possible

The reward at the end of the finest short walk in the area. Cala Pedrosa cannot be reached by road at all — you get there along the camí de ronda heading south out of Tamariu, a narrow path that climbs through pine and holm oak with the sea flashing below, then drops down a steep final scramble into a tiny shingle cove hemmed in by cliffs.

It is small, quiet and dazzlingly clear — a favourite of snorkellers and of boats that anchor just offshore. There is no sand to speak of, so bring a mat, and wear proper shoes for the descent; the last section is genuinely steep and unsuitable for young children or anyone unsteady. Go early, take water, and you may well have it almost to yourself.

Foot access onlyExcellent snorkellingQuietSteep final descentBring water
P

Parking

No parkingNo road access

There is no parking at Cala Pedrosa — the cove is reached only on foot along the camí de ronda.

Nearest free option: leave the car in Tamariu (upper village streets) and walk the coastal path south, about 35–40 minutes. From the Llafranc side, park free near the Far de Sant Sebastià approach and walk north.

Where to eat

  • A small summer chiringuito sometimes operates at the cove in high season — charming, but don't rely on it. Check locally before setting off.
  • Back in Tamariu — the obvious plan is to walk out in the morning and come back for a long late lunch at El Palanquí or Royal.

The Famous Turquoise Bay

Aiguablava

Begur · fine white sand · includes Fornells & Platgeta

From Tamariu ~2.2 km By car 6 min

"Blue water", and it earns the name — a short, sheltered bay of pale sand where the sea turns an almost implausible turquoise over the light seabed. It is the most celebrated cove on this stretch of coast and appears on half the postcards in the region, with pine-covered slopes and white villas rising steeply on either side.

The main beach is compact and gets very full in August, but the water is calm and shallow, ideal for children and for snorkelling around the edges. Next door, the smaller inlet of Fornells has a tiny harbour and its own charm, and the state-run Parador hotel occupies the headland above with a spectacular outlook. Boat trips run from here along the coast in summer.

Turquoise waterCalm & shallowBoat tripsBusy in AugustDiving
P

Parking

PaidSmall paid car park at the cove

A small pay-and-display car park sits at the entrance to the cove, under 100 m from the sand. In July and August it is full between 09:00 and 10:00.

Free option: there is limited free roadside parking on the approach above Fornells — arrive early. Otherwise Begur runs a €1 beach shuttle from the town car parks to Aiguablava, Sa Riera and Sa Tuna through the summer.

Where to eat

  • Toc al Mar — right on the sand at Aiguablava and something of a legend: fish and seafood grilled over oak charcoal, rice dishes finished in a wood oven. You can even arrive by boat. Book well ahead.
  • Hotel Aiguablava — relaxed terrace dining just above the bay.
  • Parador de Aiguablava — on the headland, with an unmatched panorama; good for a drink at sunset even if you don't eat.

Tiny Cove · Locals' Secret

Aigua Xelida

Tamariu, Palafrugell · pebble & rock platforms

From Tamariu ~3 km By car 7–8 min On foot ~40 min

The name means "chilled water" in old Catalan, after the cold freshwater springs that seep into the bay — and it is noticeably cooler than the beaches either side of it. Aigua Xelida is really a cluster of tiny pebble inlets and flat rock platforms just north of Tamariu, backed by a scatter of low white villas and a great many pines. There is a small slipway and a handful of moored boats, and that is about the extent of the development.

There are no facilities at all, which is exactly the point. Come with a towel, a mask and a picnic, claim a rock, and swim in some of the clearest water on the coast. Parking is limited to a few roadside spaces so arrive early or walk in. Best in the morning; the light on the water here at around 9am is remarkable.

No facilitiesSnorkellingVery quietRock swimmingLimited parking
P

Parking

FreeFree street parking

Free parking on Carrer de l'Avi Xicu and the surrounding streets of the Aigua Xelida urbanisation, then a five to ten minute walk down steps to the cove.

Spaces are few and unsupervised — in summer aim to arrive before 09:30. There is no other parking closer.

Where to eat

  • Nothing at the cove — bring a picnic, or plan lunch elsewhere.
  • Tamariu (5 min drive) — El Palanquí, Royal, Bar Ona, or a pizza from El Camí de Ronda to take back with you.
  • Toc al Mar, Aiguablava (5 min) — the natural pairing if you want a proper lunch after a morning swim here.
A note on parking: Almost every cove on this coast has far less parking than it has admirers. In July and August the golden rule is simple — arrive before 10am or after 4pm, or take the seasonal beach buses. We're happy to advise on the day.

5–15 Minutes by Car

The Palafrugell Coast

Tamariu's two sister villages and the wild little cove between them. All three are linked to Tamariu by the camí de ronda, so you can walk one way and take a taxi or the bus back.

Elegant Promenade · Sailing

Llafranc

Palafrugell · Blue Flag · fine golden sand

From Tamariu ~4 km By car 8–10 min Coastal walk ~1 hr

The grandest of the three Palafrugell villages — a wide, gently curving bay of soft golden sand with a broad palm-lined promenade behind it and a small marina at the western end. Llafranc feels a touch more polished than Tamariu: white awnings, ice-cream terraces, yachts swinging at anchor, and a long line of restaurants where you can sit over lunch and watch the whole bay.

It's a fine beach for swimming and for children, with plenty of space even in August, and it's the best local base for sailing, windsurfing and paddleboarding. Above the village sits the Sant Sebastià lighthouse — the drive or walk up is worth it for one of the great views of the Costa Brava, and there's a bar and restaurant at the top.

Large sandy beachPromenadeWatersportsMarinaGood for familiesSunset views
P

Parking

Free & paidFree car parks a short walk back

The blue zone along the seafront is paid from June to September. There is also a paid car park about two minutes' walk from the beach.

Free option: two free public car parks at Carrer de la Gavina 34 and Carrer de Virgili 21, plus free street parking on Carrer de la Sirena and the streets above. Outside June–September the blue zone is free.

Where to eat

  • Casamar — Michelin-starred, on the hillside above the bay. Refined modern Catalan cooking with a stunning terrace view. Reserve well ahead.
  • Restaurant Llevant — long-established seafront dining room; classic rice dishes and fresh fish.
  • Chez Tomás — a Llafranc institution just off the front, popular for grilled meats and generous Catalan plates.
  • Terrace bars on the passeig — perfect for a vermut before lunch or a gin and tonic at dusk.
  • Far de Sant Sebastià — restaurant and bar at the lighthouse; go for the panorama.

Postcard Fishing Village

Calella de Palafrugell

Palafrugell · several small coves — Port Bo, El Canadell, Port Pelegrí

From Tamariu ~6 km By car 12–14 min Coastal walk from Llafranc 25 min

The image most people carry away from the Costa Brava: whitewashed fishermen's houses with arched arcades (Els Voltes) standing directly over the water at Port Bo, boats drawn up on the shingle, and a string of small sandy coves connected by little tunnels and stepped alleyways. It is genuinely beautiful and, remarkably, has been kept almost entirely free of modern building.

Rather than one beach, Calella is a series of them — El Canadell is the biggest and best for sand and space, Port Bo the prettiest and most photographed, Port Pelegrí quieter with good rocks for snorkelling. In early July the arcades host the Cantada d'Havaneres, a night of sea shanties and burnt rum that is one of the highlights of the Catalan summer. Don't miss the Cap Roig botanical gardens on the headland above.

Several covesVery picturesqueRestaurants on the waterHavaneres festivalCap Roig gardens
P

Parking

Free & paidFree the further up you go

The car park nearest the beach has fewer than 100 spaces and the streets closest to the water are blue zone (paid).

Free option: free street parking higher up the road towards Canadell — generally a 5–10 minute walk back down. Palafrugell also runs a large free car park at Avinguda Josep Vergés i Mata (P11 'Autovia'), about 10 minutes away by car.

Where to eat

  • Tragamar — right on the sand at El Canadell; modern seafood and superb rice dishes with your feet almost in the water. Book ahead.
  • La Blava — seafront terrace at Port Bo with a lovely outlook over the arches and boats.
  • Sant Roc — the hotel restaurant on the cliff, with arguably the finest terrace view in Calella.
  • Can Batlle — a leafy, long-standing garden restaurant a little back from the front; classic Empordà cooking.

Wild Cove Below Cap Roig

El Golfet

Calella de Palafrugell · coarse sand & shingle

From Tamariu ~7 km By car ~14 min Walk from Calella 20 min

Tucked beneath the red cliffs of Cap Roig at the southern end of Calella, El Golfet is the wilder alternative to its polished neighbours. A flight of steps drops down through pine woods to a small cove of coarse sand and shingle backed by dramatic rust-coloured rock — the "red cape" that gives the headland its name.

There are no sunbeds, no bars and no music, just clear deep water that's excellent for swimming and snorkelling, and a good deal of shade in the afternoon. Because of the steps it stays quieter than Calella even in August. Combine it with a morning at the Cap Roig gardens directly above, whose terraced grounds and clifftop views are one of the great sights of the coast.

Steps downNo facilitiesSnorkellingAfternoon shadeNear Cap Roig gardens
P

Parking

FreeFree street parking above the cove

Free parking in the Cala El Golfet urbanisation around Carrer del Cap de Planes, then down a flight of steps to the beach. It is roadside parking rather than a proper car park, and it fills early in high season.

Alternative: the free Cap Roig car park, or park in Calella and walk the camí de ronda (about 20 minutes).

Where to eat

  • Nothing on the beach — take a picnic.
  • Cap Roig gardens café — a short walk up; drinks and light lunch with a view over the cove.
  • Calella de Palafrugell (20 min walk) — Tragamar, La Blava and the rest are all within easy reach.

15–25 Minutes by Car

The Begur Coves

North of Tamariu the coast breaks into a run of small, dramatic bays below the town of Begur. These are among the most photographed spots in Catalunya — and, in high summer, among the busiest. Go early.

Begur's Largest Beach

Sa Riera

Begur · sand & shingle

From Tamariu ~13 km By car ~20 min

The biggest and most practical of Begur's beaches — an old fishing cove that has grown into a small resort without losing its shape. There's a decent expanse of sand, boats hauled up at one end, a slipway, showers, sunbed hire and a proper handful of restaurants, which makes it the easiest of the Begur coves for a full day out with a family.

The water is clear and the bay reasonably sheltered, though it can pick up a swell in an easterly. Walk north around the headland and you reach Cala Illa Roja, a strikingly beautiful red-rock cove that is one of the coast's best-known naturist beaches, and beyond it the long sands of Pals.

Good facilitiesFamily-friendlySunbed hireWalk to Illa RojaParking nearby
P

Parking

PaidPaid car park at the cove

The car park above the beach is paid and small — roughly €3 an hour — and the approach road is narrow. Come early or late in the day.

Free option: use the free car parks on the edge of Begur town and take the €1 beach shuttle down (roughly June to September).

Where to eat

  • Restaurant Sa Riera — traditional seafood and rice a few steps from the sand.
  • Hotel Sa Riera — terrace lunches with a view over the bay.
  • Beach chiringuitos — a couple of summer bars on the sand for drinks and something quick.

Small, Perfect, Pebbly

Sa Tuna

Begur · pebble & shingle

From Tamariu ~13 km By car ~22 min

Many people's favourite cove on the whole Costa Brava, and it's easy to see why. Sa Tuna is a tiny horseshoe of smooth pebbles enclosed by a tight ring of old white fishermen's houses, with a single waterfront hostal-restaurant, a scatter of boats and almost nothing else. The scale of it is what charms — everything is within thirty paces of the water.

The swimming is excellent: deep, clear and immediately good for snorkelling around the rocky sides. Bring water shoes if you're tender-footed, as it's pebble rather than sand. Parking is genuinely difficult — there is a car park above the cove and a steep walk down, and in August it fills early. A short walk round the point brings you to the even smaller Aiguafreda, all rock platforms and diving-board swimming.

Pebble beachVery picturesqueDeep clear waterDifficult parkingWalk to Aiguafreda
P

Parking

PaidVery limited paid parking

The road down is narrow and winding with very few spaces, all paid. In summer you may simply not find a spot.

Free option: park free in Begur town and take the €1 beach shuttle — for Sa Tuna this is much the easier choice.

Where to eat

  • Hostal Sa Tuna — the only place at the cove and all the better for it: tapas, paella and seafood on a rustic terrace directly above the beach. Reserve in season. Passeig de l'Àncora 14, Sa Tuna · +34 972 622 198
  • Up in Begur town (10 min) — a good choice of restaurants around the castle and the old streets if you want dinner after a swim.

25–30 Minutes by Car

The Long Sands

When you want room to walk, throw a ball, or swim without company, head a little further out. These are the wide beaches — two north towards the Empordà plain, one south beyond Palamós.

Wide Open Sand · Windsurfing

Platja de Pals

Pals · ~3 km of fine golden sand

From Tamariu ~20 km By car ~30 min

A complete change of character from the coves — nearly three kilometres of open golden sand backed by umbrella pines and low dunes, running north towards the mouth of the River Ter. On a clear day you can see the Medes Islands and the great sweep of the Bay of Roses. There is always space here, even at the height of August.

The openness means more wind and more surf than the sheltered bays, which makes Pals the local centre for windsurfing, kitesurfing and paddleboarding, and a fine place for a long barefoot walk at either end of the day. Combine it with the medieval hill town of Pals itself, five minutes inland — one of the most beautifully preserved villages in Catalunya — or with the golf courses just behind the beach.

Long sandy beachSpace even in AugustWindsurf & kitesurfDunes & pinesGood parkingNear medieval Pals
P

Parking

FreeFree parking near the beach

Free parking areas serve the beach, with additional free spaces in the lower part of Pals near the sports pavilion.

The beach is long and there are several access points — the further north you go, the easier parking tends to be.

Where to eat

  • Sa Punta — the smart choice, in gardens near the southern end of the beach; polished market cuisine and a large poolside terrace. C/ Sa Punta d'es Forn 20, Platja de Pals
  • Beach chiringuitos — several along the sand in season for grilled fish, salads and cold beer, feet in the sand.
  • In Pals village (5 min inland) — excellent restaurants in the old stone streets; the local rice, arròs de Pals, is the thing to order.

The Last Undeveloped Beach

Platja de Castell

Palamós · fine sand · protected natural area

From Tamariu ~17 km By car ~25 min

Something of a local legend. In 1994 the people of Palamós voted in a referendum to stop this bay being built on, and Castell remains the last major undeveloped beach on the Costa Brava — a broad half-moon of fine sand with pine woods and open farmland behind it and not a single apartment block in sight. Walking onto it feels like stepping back forty years.

The water is clean and shallow, there's plenty of room, and the whole area is now protected. On the headland at the northern end sit the excavated remains of an Iberian settlement dating from the 6th century BC, well worth the ten-minute climb for the ruins and the view. Continue on the coastal path and you reach the tiny, exquisite Cala S'Alguer with its coloured fishermen's huts.

UndevelopedProtected areaBig sandy bayIberian ruinsKayak schoolCar park & toilets
P

Parking

Free & paidFree out of season, €5 a day in summer

A large car park with around 600 spaces sits behind the beach, a few minutes' walk from the sand. In summer it is paid: €5 for the day, €3 from 14:00, €1 from 18:00.

Free option: outside high season the same car park is free. There is no closer alternative — the beach is protected and undeveloped, which is exactly its charm.

Where to eat

  • Chiringuito Number One — the beach bar on the sand; fresh fish and seafood, good bread and coffee, in a setting that's hard to beat. Summer only.
  • Chiringuito La Cometa — the second beach bar, with a wide menu and a very friendly welcome. Summer only.
  • Palamós (10 min) — the fishing port is famous for its gambes de Palamós, the finest prawns in Catalunya. Worth a dedicated trip.

Shallow, Calm, Great for Children

La Fosca

Palamós · fine sand

From Tamariu ~18 km By car ~25 min

A wide, gently shelving bay just north of Palamós, and probably the most child-friendly beach in the area. The water stays shallow a long way out and is usually flat calm, protected by the headland — you can let small children paddle without watching every second. There's a proper little seafront with bars, sunbeds, pedalos and easy parking behind.

At the northern end stand the romantic ruins of the Castell de Sant Esteve de Mar on its rocky point, and beyond that the path continues to Cala S'Alguer and on to Castell — a lovely and easy hour's coastal walk. It's a busy, cheerful, family sort of beach rather than a wild one, and on the right day that's exactly what you want.

Very shallow waterIdeal for small childrenSunbeds & pedalosEasy parkingCastle ruinsCoastal walk
P

Parking

FreeFree car park 200 m from the sand

A free car park with around 100 spaces sits roughly 200 m from the cove, plus free street parking on the roads around the bay.

It fills by about 10:00 in August — arrive early or plan on a slightly longer walk in from the streets behind.

Where to eat

  • Restaurants on the seafront — a good row of them facing the sand, doing paella, grilled fish and generous menus del dia.
  • Beach chiringuitos — several on the sand in season for a drink or a light lunch without moving.
  • Palamós old town (5 min) — the fish market and the excellent seafood restaurants around the port; try the famous local prawns.
